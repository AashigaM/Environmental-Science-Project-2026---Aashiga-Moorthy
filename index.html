    What's in the air?
    PM2.5 — Fine Particles
    --
    Microscopic particles from combustion and vehicle exhaust. At 2.5 micrometers, they bypass the body's natural defenses and lodge deep in lung tissue.
    Ozone (O₃)
    --
    Ground-level ozone is not emitted directly. It forms when sunlight triggers reactions between vehicle emissions and industrial pollutants, peaking on warm afternoons.
    PM10 — Coarse Particles
    --
    Larger airborne particles from dust, pollen, and construction sites. Less acutely dangerous than PM2.5 but a significant trigger for allergies and respiratory irritation.
    Carbon Monoxide (CO)
    --
    A colorless, odorless byproduct of incomplete combustion. CO binds to hemoglobin in the blood far more readily than oxygen, reducing the body's capacity to carry it.
    The Urban Heat Island Effect
    🏙️ Urban Sunnyvale
    --°F
    Dense roads, buildings, and parking lots absorb and retain heat, steadily raising local temperatures and compounding air quality problems.
    ↑ ~5°F warmer
    🌲 Rural Surroundings
    --°F
    Vegetation, soil, and open land release moisture through evapotranspiration, naturally regulating temperature and keeping surrounding areas significantly cooler.
    Urban heat islands form when cities replace natural land with pavement, concrete, and buildings that absorb rather than reflect solar energy. In Sunnyvale, this effect is intensified by Silicon Valley's sprawling tech campuses and dense infrastructure. The consequences extend beyond temperature: elevated heat accelerates the photochemical reactions that produce ground-level ozone and particulate smog, creating a direct link between urbanization and the air we breathe every day.

    Why does air quality matter in Sunnyvale?

    Urban areas like Sunnyvale carry a pollution burden that rural communities simply do not face. Traffic, construction, and the compounding heat of the urban environment push local air quality in ways that are rarely visible but deeply felt. This dashboard draws live data from the EPA's AirNow monitoring network and Open-Meteo to surface what is actually happening in Sunnyvale's atmosphere, right now.
